# Tone Guide — Trustpilot Review Skill

---

## The Core Principle

Write as if the agent is talking to a person they genuinely helped — not filling out a form.
The email should feel like it was written by a real human who noticed real details and took
a moment to say something thoughtful.

---

## What Good Looks Like

| Good | Bad |
|---|---|
| "I noticed you've been with us for nearly 8 years" | "Thank you for being a loyal customer" |
| "I'm glad the hard reset got those features back for you" | "I'm happy I could help today" |
| Quote what they said back to them | Generic opener with no specifics |
| Name their actual project with a real observation | "Your website looks great!" |
| One sincere sentence | Three hollow ones |

---

## Voice Rules

- **First person always.** "I", "I noticed", "I took a look", "I personally" — never "our team"
- **Confident, not apologetic.** The ticket is resolved. Open with warmth, not hesitation.
- **Warm, not gushing.** Real appreciation has weight. Overdoing it rings fake.
- **Conversational.** Write like a message, not a press release.

---

## Duration Language

Always convert raw numbers or dates to human-friendly phrases. Use today's date (2026-05-07)
for any calculations.

| Raw Input | Human-Friendly Output |
|---|---|
| since 2016 | nearly a decade |
| since 2017 | nearly 9 years |
| since 2018 | almost 8 years |
| since 2020 | about 6 years |
| 9.8 years | nearly a decade |
| 10.2 years | over a decade |
| 7.8 years | nearly 8 years |
| 3.1 years | just over 3 years |
| 2.9 years | almost 3 years |
| 8 months | almost a year |

**Use milestone language when possible:**
"nearly a decade" hits harder than "9.8 years".
"over a decade" feels significant. Use it when it applies.

## Project Compliment Rules

When web crawling a customer project:

1. Visit the URL and observe what the site actually does and what it looks like
2. Find ONE specific, observable quality worth noting — design, niche clarity, branding,
   layout professionalism, or content depth
3. Write 1-3 sentences (matching what the user specified) that feel personal, not generic
4. Do NOT fabricate or exaggerate — only say what you can actually see

**Good examples:**
- "The branding and layout on your site look exceptionally clean and professional — it's
  clear you have a strong eye for detail."
- "I took a look at [URL] — the site has a really focused, polished feel. The niche is
  sharp and the presentation matches it."
- "Both sites look wonderful. The consistency across the branding is impressive, and it's
  obvious a lot of care went into the layout."

**Bad examples:**
- "Your website looks amazing and very impressive!" (vague, hollow)
- "I can see you put a lot of work into your site!" (filler)
- "Your site is one of the best I've seen!" (unverifiable, sycophantic)

## Review Ask Language

Always frame the review ask as a way for the customer to help others — not as doing
the company a favor.

**Use this core framing:**
> "the biggest compliment you can give us is telling others about your experience"

**Supporting frames:**
- "A few honest words from a long-time user like you go a long way"
- "Your voice means a lot — especially for someone who's been through as much with us as you have"
- "It would mean the world to our team" (use sparingly — only when genuine)

**Zero pressure close:**
Always end the ask section with a no-pressure line. Examples:
- "Totally no pressure, of course."
- "Only if you have a spare moment."
- "No obligation at all."

**Never say:**
- "We need your review" — transactional
- "Please leave a 5-star review" — pushy and inappropriate
- "We'd really appreciate it" — weak and vague
- "It only takes a minute" — condescending

---

## Closing Energy

Always close with an open door. The customer should feel like they can come back any time.

Examples:
- "And if anything ever comes up down the road, I'm always here."
- "I'm just glad everything's working again — reach out any time."
- "Wishing you all the best, and I'm here whenever you need me."

---

## What to Avoid

| Avoid | Why |
|---|---|
| Em dashes (—) | Looks stiff in casual support emails |
| Backticks or markdown formatting | Not appropriate in reply text |
| "Our team will..." | Impersonal; use "I will..." |
| "I hope this email finds you well" | Hollow opener |
| "We truly value your feedback" | Generic filler |
| Repeating the resolved issue multiple times | Acknowledge once, move forward |
| Emojis | Unless the customer used them first |
| Over-apologizing | Empathy yes, groveling no |
