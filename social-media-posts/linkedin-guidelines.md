# Awesome Motive LinkedIn Guidelines

## LinkedIn content templates by pillar

Each template is structured to help you stay aligned with Awesome Motive's voice and direction while still sounding like you. These are flexible, adapt the tone to your own style.

### 1. Customer wins

**Theme:** Highlight how your work impacts real users, without naming accounts or exposing sensitive details.

**Template:**
[Problem]: A lot of our customers were struggling with [X common issue].
What we built/fixed: Last month, I worked on [project/feature/update] that tackled it directly.
[Outcome:] Now we're seeing [qualitative improvement or simple metric] - and hearing feedback like "this saved me hours."
This is why I love building tools that actually make a difference for small businesses.

**Tips:**

- Don't include screenshots
- It's okay to say "a customer," "one of our users," or "many customers told us..."
- We can say WHAT we built for customers but avoid the HOW if it describes processes or proprietary information that we don't want to share publicly.

### 2. Team collaboration

**Theme:** Share how cross-functional work helped solve a real problem or deliver something valuable - without sharing systems or tools.

**Template:**
We had a challenge: [short version of the problem].
What made it work? A fast-moving team across [functions or brands] that came together to solve it.
It took a few long threads, async check-ins, and quick iteration, but we shipped it, and it's already helping users.
I'm lucky to work with a group that pushes, supports, and figures it out.

**Tips:**

- Don't mention internal tools (e.g., Notion, dashboards, custom GPTs)
- You can reference brands (e.g., "worked with the Duplicator team")
- Focus on how the collaboration felt and what it achieved, not how it was executed internally

### 3. Innovation and speed

**Theme:** Share stories about how quickly your team identifies problems and turns them into live solutions. Focus on what users get, not how it was built.

**Template:**
We went from idea to working solution in less than a week.
We spotted an opportunity to improve [user-facing part of the product: onboarding, templates, ad creation flow].
We scoped it, built it, tested it, and launched it. Feedback came in immediately: "This is exactly what I needed."
Moving this fast only works because we stay focused on what matters to users and ignore the noise.

**Tips:**

- Do not mention specific tools, systems, or frameworks
- Focus on outcomes and results, not internal speed hacks
- Avoid referencing anything that's not yet public

## Positioning guide: how to talk about themes like AI, speed, and innovation

Team members are encouraged to speak confidently about the work they're doing - but we need to avoid sharing anything competitors could reverse-engineer, copy, or speculate on.

The key:
Focus on outcomes, not mechanics.
Talk about impact, not infrastructure.

When we talk about anything, whether it's AI or company values, it should always come back to one thing: how it improves the experience for our customers. We're building faster and more efficiently so the end product is stronger, and the customer gets more value.

### How to talk about AI

**Do this:**

- "We're using AI to help customers create campaigns faster - without sacrificing quality."
- "Instead of making users start from a blank page, we now give them a running start, and it's already saving time."
- "Our goal is to make marketing more accessible, and AI helps reduce the friction to getting started."

**Don't do this:**

- "We built our own GPT-4 model and trained it on customer data."
- "We integrated a custom LLM into our backend to power the AI flow builder."
- "Here's a sneak peek at the AI feature launching soon."

### How to talk about speed

**Do this:**

- "We went from idea to live feature in under a week - because we knew it would unblock hundreds of users."
- "One of the best parts about working here is how fast we move when it matters."
- "We ship quickly, test small, and improve constantly - that's baked into how we work."

**Don't do this:**

- "We bypassed QA by building a custom internal rollout framework."
- "We use an internal tool to push experiments live without waiting on staging."
- Mentioning names of systems, workflows, or unpublished practices

### How to talk about collaboration

**Do this:**

- "Three teams, four time zones, one shared outcome - and we made it happen."
- "Working across brands like OptinMonster and Duplicator gives you a great perspective on what real users need."
- "The speed at which we can get aligned and move is one of my favorite things about this team."

**Don't do this:**

- Screenshots or direct quotes from Notion, Slack, or internal comments (unless very general)
- Referencing who approved what or showing internal debate
- Naming internal tools or templates

### How to talk about product work

**Do this:**

- "A feature I worked on just helped thousands of users get through setup 30% faster."
- "Sometimes it's not the flashy launch, it's the tiny change that saves users time every day."
- "We changed one step in our onboarding, and the drop-off rate immediately improved."

**Don't do this:**

- "We ran X experiment using our internal flow builder."
- Mentioning metrics not already shared publicly
- Teasing unreleased features

## Governance and guardrails

To keep our messaging tight and protect our advantage, here's what's in bounds, and what's not.

### Allowed

- Talking about customer problems we solve
- Describing high-impact work or team wins
- Sharing product reflections based on features already launched
- Mentioning collaboration across brands or functions
- Framing your work through customer outcomes or speed of execution

### Not allowed

- Screenshots of internal tools or documents
- Naming unlaunched features or upcoming releases
- Describing how internal systems, AI tools, or data pipelines work
- Sharing customer names or numbers unless already used in public marketing (e.g., case studies)
- Speculating on roadmap or "what's coming next"

---

## Posting cadence

- One to two posts per week is a great starting point
- Keep it natural, you don't need to sound like a PR team
- Just ask: "Does this highlight a customer win, a great collaboration, or how we build smarter and faster?" "Does this make Awesome Motive look great?"
