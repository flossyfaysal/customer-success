# Scenario: Platform Exit (Leaving WordPress)

## Description
The customer is leaving WordPress entirely and switching to another platform (Wix, Squarespace, Shopify, etc.). They have no dissatisfaction with the product itself — the reason for the refund is external. This is "appreciation churn": the customer leaves on good terms.

## Common Triggers
- Business decision to move off WordPress
- Client no longer requires WordPress
- Developer or agency switching their stack
- License just renewed (unfortunate timing)

## Key Characteristics
- Usually polite, low or no frustration
- Refund is often legitimate and should be honored without resistance
- No point offering WordPress-specific solutions (migration help, server config, etc.)
- The customer often had a genuinely good experience with the product

## Approach Pattern
1. **Honor the refund immediately** — no retention pitch for a product they genuinely can't use
2. **Acknowledge their loyalty specifically** — reference actual details: years with us, sites activated, usage history
3. **Express genuine appreciation** — make it personal, not a generic "thank you for being a customer"
4. **Request a Trustpilot review as a favor** — they had a good experience; this is the right moment to ask
5. **Acknowledge the platform constraint** — no discount or service offer makes sense if they're off WordPress
6. **Leave the door open warmly** — "you're always welcome back if you return to WordPress"

## Why This Approach Works
- Customers who leave due to platform switch (not dissatisfaction) become goodwill ambassadors if treated with respect
- Acknowledging specific details (6 years, multiple activations) makes appreciation feel genuine
- Asking for a Trustpilot review at this moment is natural — positive experience + graceful exit = highest chance of a yes
- The open-door close costs nothing and builds long-term brand equity

## What NOT to Do
- Don't offer WordPress-specific services if they've already left the platform
- Don't push retention too hard when the reason is genuinely out of their control
- Don't give a generic "thank you for your business" — reference their actual history
